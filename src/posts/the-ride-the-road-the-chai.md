Imagine this for a moment. It's early morning, the kind of morning where the air still carries a little coolness and the world hasn't fully rushed into the day yet. Your motorbike is parked outside, slightly dusty from the last ride but ready to go again. You put on your helmet, turn the key, and the engine wakes up with that familiar sound that always brings a small smile to your face. There is no real rush today and no strict plan waiting for you. Maybe you have a town in mind somewhere a few hours away, maybe you don't. That uncertainty doesn't bother you, because the destination has never been the most exciting part. The ride itself has always been the reason you start the engine.

The first few kilometers pass through a slowly waking city. A few shops are opening their shutters, buses begin their routes, and people walk along the streets carrying the small responsibilities of a new day. But as you continue riding, the buildings slowly thin out and the road begins to open wider. The traffic fades, the air feels lighter, and soon the city is behind you. What lies ahead now are long state highways stretching across fields, small villages, and quiet patches of trees. The road moves steadily forward, and with every passing kilometer, the ride begins to feel calmer and freer.

As the bike settles into its rhythm, the sound of the engine becomes steady and comforting. The wind brushes past your shoulders and helmet, and for a while you stop thinking about everything else. There is something about a long ride that quietly clears the mind. The worries that seemed important earlier in the day lose their urgency, replaced by the simple awareness of the road ahead, the sky above, and the constant motion of the journey.

Every now and then you slow down just a little, not because you need to, but because there is always something worth noticing. A group of farmers working in the fields beside the highway. A herd of goats crossing the road with the confidence of creatures that believe the road belongs to them. A small temple painted in bright colors with a few flags fluttering gently in the morning breeze. These are the moments that quietly become part of the ride, the small scenes that you might never remember clearly but somehow always carry with you.

---

After riding for an hour or two, you begin to feel the familiar need for a short break. Not because you are tired, but because a ride always feels incomplete without one of those small stops along the way. Soon enough, you spot exactly what you were hoping for — a tiny roadside tapri standing beside the highway. It has a few plastic chairs, a simple counter, and a kettle that seems to have been boiling continuously since early morning. Places like these appear without warning and disappear just as easily once you ride past them, but they always feel welcoming.

You park the bike nearby and walk toward the stall. The conversation begins the way it almost always does. "Bhaiya, chai kitne ki?" The tapri guy tells you the price and pours the tea into a small glass, handing it to you with the casual familiarity that comes from serving travelers all day. But before you even take the first sip, his attention drifts toward the motorcycle parked outside. Curiosity slowly replaces routine, and the next question comes naturally. "Ye bike kitni cc hai?"

You smile because you have heard that question many times before. Soon both of you are standing near the bike, talking about the engine, the mileage, and the places you have ridden. Sometimes a truck driver joins the conversation and shares his own highway stories. Sometimes a curious kid stands nearby, quietly studying every detail of the motorcycle like it is the most fascinating machine he has seen all week. The tea itself is simple and the stall is small, but the conversation somehow makes the stop feel special.

---

Soon the tea glass becomes empty, and the short pause that felt so comfortable begins to come to an end. You stretch your arms a little, put your helmet back on, and walk back toward the bike parked beside the stall. The tapri guy gives a small nod as you start the engine again and says, "Safe ride," the way people on highways often do, like a small blessing for the road ahead. You wave once before easing the bike back onto the highway, letting it settle again into that familiar rhythm of motion.

The road stretches ahead quietly, welcoming in its own simple way. You don't really know where you will stop next, and that uncertainty is part of the charm. Maybe it will be a small town you have never heard of before, or maybe just another roadside chai stall somewhere further down the highway where the kettle is already boiling and a few strangers are waiting for their next conversation. But that is the beauty of rides like these. The destination never feels as important as the miles between here and there, because the real joy is knowing that the road is still open, the engine is still humming, and somewhere ahead another small stop is waiting to become part of the journey.
